Who Am I?

An Environmental Engineer with passion for Geophysics, driven by venturing into nature and analyzing the data it provides.

Current Research and Academic Background

I am a researcher primarily focused on Hydrology and Environmental Sciences. My experience includes field instrumentation, data collection, numerical modeling, and data analysis. Additionally, I taught several engineering courses over six years and offered Python programming courses.

Currently, I am running my Hydrological Modelling research at the University of Idaho at the Dr. Meng Zhao's group. I am conducting a study on groundwater and evapotranspiration modeling in a project with U.S. Geological Survey (USGS). This project aims to improve the plant-hydraulic modeling processes description on the influence of groundwater on evapotranspiration and ultimately in the water balance over the Contiguous United States (CONUS).

My research interests revolve around studying data-driven hydrological processes through remote-sensing, eddy-covariance flux data, and field work instrumentation.

Recent Research

Here are the main publications I developed under my PhD studies.

Evaluation of the storage and evapotranspiration terms of the water budget for an agricultural watershed using local and remote-sensing measurements.

(Hoeltgebaum, Dias, and Costa, 2021)

In this article, we evaluate the impact of energy balance closure on the water budget, as well as the influence of soil moisture spatial variability on basin-scale evapotranspiration estimates using the water-budget method. See full details in our publication in Agricultural and Forest Meteorology.

An analog period method for gap-filling of latent heat flux measurements.

(Hoeltgebaum and Dias, 2023)

A new gap-filling method for latent heat flux, adapted from a wind-speed predictor approach. The Analog Period Method (APM) is based on the idea that missing data can be filled by identifying and applying patterns from existing periods before and after the gap. It searches for similar periods within the dataset to reconstruct the missing values. Take a look in our results at Hydrological Processes.

STAEBLE: A surface‐temperature‐and available‐energy‐based lake evaporation model.

(Dias, Hoeltgebaum, and Santos, 2023)

A simple lake evaporation model calibrated using satellite and land-based data. This mass transfer model uses MODIS water temperature and land-based meteorological data, calibrating the transfer coefficient through long-term energy budget closure. Tested at Lake Mead, it accounts for atmospheric stability and performs well without requiring local measurements. Check the paper here.
